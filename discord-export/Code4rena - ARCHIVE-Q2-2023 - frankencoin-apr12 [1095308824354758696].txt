[04/12/2023 22:50] levijohnson (pinned)
hi @reentrant , the goal of the auction mechanism is to determine the fair price of the collateral, so that Frankencoin (XCHF) is always sufficiently backed and the system remains in balance. 

Yes, there are more than 2 actors: The position owner, the challenger and the bidders. 

Here's a slightly, but hopefully more conclusive, description from the frankecoin.com app: 

"Collateralized minting positions allow their owner to mint ZCHF against a collateral. Anyone can open new collateral positions and start minting ZCHF once the initialization period has passed. Positions that are not sufficiently collateralized can be challenged by anyone through an auction mechanism. When challenging a position, the challenger must provide some of the collateral to be auctioned off. If the highest bid in the subsequent auction is high enough to show that the position is sufficiently collateralized, the challenge is averted and the bidder gets the challengers collateral in exchange for the highest bid. If the highest bid is lower, the challenge is considered successful, the bidder gets the collateral from the position and the position is closed, distributing excess proceeds to the reserve and paying a reward to the challenger."

[04/13/2023 00:04] hermel007 (pinned)
In case you wonder about the differences. From the top of my mind, the most important ones are:
- "price" in the paper refers to the market price, whereas the variable position.price in the implementation refers to the liquidation price.
- the minter reserve was adjusted after the paper was written and the equity share allocation is completely different. It is probably best to ignore section 3 of the paper entirely and to instead refer to the gitbook documentation and the code comments.
- in the paper, the perspective is that you borrow 80 ZCHF but get liquidated once the collateral is worth less than (1+h)*80 = 100. In the implementation, the perspective is that you borrow the 100 ZCHF, but only get to use 80 as 20 is allocated to the reserve.
- the paper does not care about timing questions between providing the collateral and actually minting ZCHF. It assumes that these always happen together, whereas in the implementation, providing collateral and minting ZCHF are two separate steps.

[04/16/2023 11:12] hermel007
If the challenge fails, that means that there was a bidder that is willing to pay the liquidation price for the collateral. In that case, the bidder gets the collateral provided by the challenger and the challenger gets the bid. The owner of the position is completely unaffected.
This changes if the position has expired: in that case, every challenge succeeds which essentially means that the collateral is sold to the highest bidder and the system makes a nice profit.
